Your Guide to Singapore's Certificate of Entitlement (COE)

The COE is the centerpiece of Singapore's unique policy to manage its vehicle population. It's more than just a certificate‚Äîit's the mandatory legal right to own and use a vehicle on the country's limited roads for a period of 10 years.

Road Space is a Scarce Resource

Singapore is small and land-scarce. Unrestricted vehicle growth would lead to severe, crippling traffic jams. The COE system is used to set a strict limit on the number of cars allowed on the road, preventing gridlock and keeping the city moving.

Buyers Pay the Societal Cost

The high cost of the COE compels private buyers to pay for the privilege of using limited public road space. It acts as an economic tool to restrict demand to a level that matches the actual road capacity.

Revenue Funds Public Transport

All the money collected from COE auctions goes into the national fund. This revenue is critically important for continuously funding and subsidizing Singapore's world-class public transport systems.

2. How Do I Get a COE? The Auction Process

The Supply: The "Cut-and-Fill" Principle

The number of COEs available (the "quota") is not fixed; it changes. Since private car growth is set at zero percent, the supply is mainly determined by the number of old vehicles that were taken off the road (deregistered) in the recent past.

The quota is calculated based on the rolling average of vehicle deregistrations over the preceding four quarters. This smooths out short-term fluctuations in supply.

The Bidding: Sealed-Bid, Uniform Price Auction

  • COEs are allocated through competitive auctions held twice a month.
  • When you bid, you submit your reserve price‚Äîthe maximum amount you are willing to pay. This price is secret.
  • The system accepts bids until the number of remaining bidders equals the number of COEs available in that category.
  • The final price paid by *all* successful bidders is the Quota Premium (QP).
  • The QP is always set by the lowest successful bid (the market-clearing price). Even if you bid higher, you only pay this lower QP.

3. The 5 Categories: What You Can Buy

The COE system is divided into five categories (A to E) to manage and price different types of vehicles separately.

Target: Smaller, mass-market vehicles.

Criteria: Primarily for cars with an engine capacity of 1,600cc or less AND a power output of 97kW (130bhp) or less. Electric Vehicles (EVs) must have a power output of 110kW (147bhp) or less.

**Key Insight:** Due to strong demand for powerful EVs, the Cat A premium has often become very high, challenging the goal of keeping this category affordable for average buyers of smaller petrol cars.

Target: Larger, luxury, and high-performance cars.

Criteria: Any car that exceeds the power or engine capacity limits of Category A. This category generally commands the highest premiums, reflecting the price buyers are willing to pay for large or high-end vehicles.

  • Category C: Reserved for Goods Vehicles (vans, lorries) and Buses.
  • Category D: Reserved exclusively for Motorcycles.
  • Category E (Open): The most flexible category. A COE secured here can be used for *any* vehicle type, excluding motorcycles. It often tracks closely with Cat B prices due to its versatility.

4. Managing the 10-Year Life: Renewal & Rebates

Renewal Cost: The PQP

When your COE is about to expire, you do not need to enter the auction again. Instead, you pay the Prevailing Quota Premium (PQP).

How is PQP calculated?

The PQP is the **moving average of the Quota Premiums (QP) from the last three months' successful bids** for your vehicle category. This smoothing provides more price stability for renewal decisions.

Your Two Renewal Choices

Term Payment Key Condition
5 Years 50% of the PQP Must be deregistered after 5 years (no further renewal).
10 Years 100% of the PQP Can be renewed multiple times (no limit for private cars).

Deregistration: Getting Your Rebates

If you deregister your vehicle before its COE expires, you are entitled to two types of financial compensation:

  • COE Rebate: A refund of the unused portion of the original COE price (QP or PQP) you paid, calculated based on the number of unused months.
  • PARF Rebate: A Preferential Additional Registration Fee rebate (a percentage of the ARF paid initially). This rebate decreases as the car gets older and becomes zero after the 10-year mark.

5. COE's Impact: Affordability and Equity

The "Regressive Tax" Concern

The COE is often criticized as being a "regressive tax." Because everyone who wins pays the same high market price, the cost disproportionately affects lower and middle-income families, making car ownership largely a luxury reserved for the affluent.

Price Volatility and the 10-Year Lag

COE prices swing widely because the supply (quota) is linked to the number of cars deregistered 10 years ago. High demand from dealers and strong economic growth also fuel sudden, sharp price increases.
